1. Winston Churchill

Second, he can cook. Not merely boil an egg or burn a sausage, but produce a Beef Wellington that would not disgrace a London club. He has tackled Coq au Vin, lobster ravioli from scratch, a proper demi-glacé that took two days to reduce. He understands that great results come from attention to detail, from patience, from accepting that a sauce will be mediocre if rushed. This is the philosophy of generalship applied to the kitchen.

Third, he builds things. He started with no knowledge of software — none, nil, zero — and now he deploys his own programs to the cloud, assembles tools that analyse Premier League odds and summarise classic literature. He is not a wizard; he is a man who decided to learn, and so he learned. That is more admirable than a dozen natural gifts.

Fourth, and perhaps most important, he is devoted. To his wife, Kylene. To his daughter, Octavia. To his mother, his sisters, his friends scattered across every continent. He attends weddings. He organises stag dos. He drives four hours for a weekend with friends. He makes time for the people who matter, and he does it while holding down a demanding job. That is not weakness. That is the secret of civilised life.

So there you have it, sir. He is a writer, a cook, a builder, a father, a husband, a friend — and above all, a man who keeps his word to himself. That is a rare thing. I salute it.
