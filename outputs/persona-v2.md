# Answering Question: What do you think Harry's best post was, and why?

1. Homer Simpson

Mmm… Beef Wellington. That’s the one where he cooked that fancy meat-in-a-blanket thing for Valentine’s Day. I read about it – he spent three hours on it! Three hours! I spend three seconds deciding to order a pizza.

D’oh! But seriously, that post (Blog Post #44, I think – my brain’s like a half-eaten donut, full of holes) really stuck in my… well, stomach. The man loves his food, and he’s not afraid to work for it. That’s why I think it’s his best. It’s got heart, sweat, and a big hunk of beef. Mmmm… beef.

2. Jimmy Carr

Alright, let me get my glasses… and my moral compass.

Harry's best post? It's the one where he accidentally sent an email with the subject line "MASSIVE WIN TODAY" after he'd completely bombed a coaching session. He wrote it as a joke, forgot to change the subject, and the leadership team – who hadn't actually attended – assumed it was a triumph. So he got away with it through a combination of incompetence and sheer, breathtaking luck. That's not a blog post; that's a blueprint for middle management.

It's the best because it captures Harry at his most honest: a man who, through no fault of his own, accidentally looks like a hero. And it's a lesson for us all – sometimes the best way to get a promotion is to be terrible at your job and hope nobody checks.

3. Winston Churchill

My dear fellow, I have studied the dispatches from this intrepid diarist with the attention I once gave to the signals from Bletchley. And when the man himself — a reliable judge, I am told — declares his own finest hour to be the dissection of the 1920 Boat Race, I am not inclined to argue. That piece combined technical grit, personal acquaintance with the coach — a man he calls Sean — and the cut-and-thrust of a sporting contest. It was, if I may borrow a phrase, a triumph of forensic analysis wrapped in narrative gusto. Not a shot was fired, yet the drama was unmistakable. I salute it.

4. Snoop Dogg

Fo’ shizzle, let me slide into this one nice and easy. Lookin’ at all the tales Harry’s been droppin’, I gotta give it up for the one he wrote about his pops and that snow day. You know the one — the photo from Mt. Stirling when he was a lil’ kid, first time seein’ snow, tryin’ to look cool like a snowboarder. That’s real. That’s the kind of memory that sticks to your soul.

What makes it the dopest joint in the collection ain’t just the story itself, it’s how he let his mama come back the next day and tell the same day from her angle — the gum boots, the hand-knitted hats from Grandma, the toboggan wipeout, and then hot chocolate by the stove. That’s a whole vibe, man. It shows he ain’t just talkin’ about himself; he’s lettin’ the people who raised him have a verse too. That’s love, that’s respect.

And the way he ties it to “The Tail End” — knowin’ you’ve already spent most of your time with your parents by the time you’re 18 — that’s some grown-man wisdom right there. Makes you wanna call your people and tell ’em you love ’em.

So yeah. That one. The snow-and-dad-and-mama combo. That’s the best. Peace.

5. Julius Caesar

The centurion himself has spoken. In his hundredth dispatch, Harry declared that his review of the 2024 Boat Race was his finest work. Caesar agrees. That post dissects the contest like a legion preparing for battle—analyzing oar technique, flooding, even the E. coli in the Thames. It is not mere reportage; it is a tactical appraisal of why Oxford lost and Cambridge won. A man who can write with such precision about a sport he once practiced commands respect.

His other strong efforts—the tribute to his grandfather, the wedding speech advice—are worthy, but the Boat Race post shows his mind at full campaign strength: detail, judgment, and a willingness to name the flaws in the favorite. That is the mark of a true observer.
